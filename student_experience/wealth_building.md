# Wealth Building for PhD Students

## Overview

Building financial security on a graduate stipend is challenging but possible. The key is starting early — even small, consistent contributions to an emergency fund and retirement account compound significantly over a 5–7 year PhD program. This guide covers budgeting, saving, investing, and managing debt on a grad-school income.

---

## 1. Key Financial Milestones for Graduate Students

Work through these roughly in order:

1. **Build a $1,000 starter emergency fund** — protects against unexpected car repairs, medical bills, etc.
2. **Maximize employer retirement match** — if your university offers any match on a 403(b) or 457(b), contribute at least enough to capture the full match (free money).
3. **Pay off high-interest debt** (>7% APR) — credit cards and private student loans.
4. **Build a 3–6 month emergency fund** — equivalent to 3–6 months of essential expenses.
5. **Contribute to a Roth IRA** — up to $7,000/year (2024 limit) if you have earned income (TA/RA W-2). Fellowship income may also qualify — confirm with a tax advisor.
6. **Reduce or refinance moderate-interest debt** (undergraduate loans, if applicable).
7. **Increase retirement contributions** in a 403(b) / 457(b) if available.

Customize the percentages for your situation. The "50/30/20" framework is a useful starting point:

| Category | Target % of Take-Home | Your Target ($/mo) | Actual ($/mo) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Needs** (housing, utilities, groceries, transport, insurance) | 50% | | |
| **Wants** (dining out, entertainment, subscriptions, hobbies) | 30% | | |
| **Savings & Debt Repayment** (emergency fund, retirement, loan payments) | 20% | | |

## 3. Retirement Accounts Available to Graduate Students

Even contributing $50–$100/month to a Roth IRA in graduate school can grow to $30,000–$50,000 by retirement (assuming 7% average annual return over 35 years).

| Account Type | Who Can Contribute | 2024 Limit | Tax Treatment | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Roth IRA** | Anyone with earned income (W-2 or qualifying fellowship income) under income limits | $7,000/yr ($8,000 if 50+) | Contributions post-tax; growth & withdrawals tax-free | Best option for most grad students (low tax bracket now) |
| **Traditional IRA** | Anyone with earned income | $7,000/yr | Contributions may be deductible; withdrawals taxed | Less advantageous than Roth at low income |
| **403(b)** | University employees (TA/RA) | $23,000/yr (2024) | Pre-tax contributions; taxed on withdrawal | Only if your university offers enrollment |
| **457(b)** | Some university employees | $23,000/yr | Pre-tax; no early withdrawal penalty | Rare but valuable if available |

> **Fellowship students:** IRS guidance on whether fellowship income qualifies as "earned income" for IRA purposes has evolved. As of 2023, qualified stipends can count. Consult [IRS Publication 590-A](https://www.irs.gov/publications/p590a) or a tax professional.

## 4. Managing Student Loan Debt

If you carry undergraduate loans into graduate school:

- **Federal loans are typically deferred** during enrollment (in-school deferment), but interest may continue accruing on unsubsidized loans.
- Consider **paying interest only** during graduate school to prevent capitalization.
- Research **income-driven repayment (IDR)** plans: SAVE, PAYE, IBR. These cap payments at a percentage of discretionary income, which can be very low (or $0) on a grad stipend.
- **Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF):** If you plan to work at a nonprofit or government employer after graduation, payments during grad school can count toward the 120 required for PSLF (if you are on an IDR plan and not in deferment). Contact your servicer to verify.

**Tools:**
- [Federal Student Aid Loan Simulator](https://studentaid.gov/loan-simulator/)
- [PSLF Help Tool](https://studentaid.gov/pslf/)

## 5. Tax Strategy for Graduate Students

| Strategy | Benefit | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Claim the **Lifetime Learning Credit** | Up to $2,000 credit on tuition & fees | Phases out at higher incomes; cannot be combined with some exclusions |
| Deduct **student loan interest** | Up to $2,500 deduction | Income limits apply |
| Contribute to **Roth IRA** | Tax-free growth | Requires earned income; file IRS Form 8606 |
| Track **unreimbursed business expenses** (conferences, supplies) | May be deductible as Schedule C expenses if self-employed (fellowship) | Keep receipts |
| File **quarterly estimated taxes** (fellowships) | Avoid underpayment penalty | Use IRS Form 1040-ES |

## 6. Building Credit During Graduate School

Good credit saves money on car loans, mortgages, and insurance premiums post-PhD.

- [ ] Open a **no-annual-fee credit card** (e.g., a student card or secured card).
- [ ] Charge a small recurring expense (e.g., streaming subscription) and pay it in full each month.
- [ ] Keep **credit utilization below 30%** of your credit limit.
- [ ] Do **not** close old accounts — length of credit history matters.
- [ ] Check your **free credit reports** annually at [AnnualCreditReport.com](https://www.annualcreditreport.com/).
